Short bio

My research studies how economic growth shapes and is shaped by environmental and climate change, and how people and firms adapt to this change. I utilise administrative and satellite-based datasets, and primary surveys in combination with reduced-form and structural methods from applied micro in my work. I am particularly interested in policy-relevant questions such as the consequences of air and water pollution for economic growth, and how anti-poverty programs affect farmers’s adaptation decisions to climate change.

Prior to the PhD program, I worked as a policy adviser to the state government of Delhi, conducted research at the World Bank, and was also a software engineer in India. I am grateful for the opportunities to study at the LSE, UC Berkeley and IIT-Dhanbad in India. Needless to say, I have been shaped by these experiences, as also by working at J-PAL, growing up in small-town India, and living in many parts of India, the US and the UK.

The Impact of Reducing Agricultural Fires on Aggregate Productivity and Spatial Inequality in India

How do consistently high levels of air pollution in some Indian cities affect aggregate output and spatial inequality? I investigate this question in the context of India through a quantitative spatial equilibrium model that features endogenous air pollution generation from both agriculture and non-agriculture, with pollution transport across locations. Using exogenous variation in yearly wind and cropping patterns, I first provide evidence that up to 23% of particulate matter concentrations (pm2.5) in Indian cities can be explained by agricultural fires. Pollution causes both amenity and productivity shocks to workers who can engage in costly migration in response. I estimate the pollution elasticity of migration that governs the disutility from air pollution using exogenous variation in daily wind patterns and fire counts, the income elasticity that governs migration response to relative income differences using instruments from trade theory, and the labor elasticity of agricultural fires using panel data. Model counterfactuals that reduce prevalence of agricultural fires in north-western India to levels in the rest of the country raise national GDP by 1.16%, with the Gini coefficient of spatial welfare reducing by 1.2%. Sorting toward downwind cities from distant cities further increases GDP in downwind cities but at minor cost to welfare gains.
